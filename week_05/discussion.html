Module 5: Discussion Topics

Higher-Order Thinking for Class Engagement

These discussion prompts are designed to move beyond technical definitions toward critical analysis, synthesis, and evaluation. Use them to spark deeper conversation about why metrics matter and what they hide.

Analysis / Ethics

1. The Politics of Precision and Recall

Precision favors caution; recall favors vigilance. This trade-off is not just mathematical—it's political. Who wins and loses in this trade-off?

  • In fraud detection, banks prefer precision (don't annoy good customers). Merchants prefer recall (catch all fraud). Who decides?
  • If a cancer screen has high recall but low precision, patients face unnecessary anxiety and procedures. Is that acceptable?
  • Is "presumption of innocence" (legal standard) essentially a precision argument?
  • Can you think of a system that should maximize recall even at the cost of many false alarms?

Try: Have students negotiate precision-recall trade-offs as different stakeholders.

Synthesis / Critical Thinking

2. Class Imbalance as a Social Phenomenon

"Imbalanced classes" is a technical term, but imbalance reflects real-world rarity. Why are some things rare, and what happens when we oversample or undersample?

  • Fraud is rare because most people are honest. If we oversample fraud, what assumptions are we making about human nature?
  • SMOTE creates synthetic minority examples. What does it mean to create synthetic fraudsters or cancer patients?
  • If we balance classes artificially, does the model learn about the real world or a fantasy?
  • Is class imbalance a problem to be solved, or information to be respected?

Try: Calculate base rates for rare events (plane crashes, lottery wins). Discuss why accurate prediction is so hard.

Evaluation / Philosophy

3. Aggregating Metrics: What Gets Hidden?

F1-score, macro-average, micro-average—we combine metrics into single numbers. What simplicity gives us, it takes away in understanding. What's hidden in aggregation?

  • If overall accuracy is 90%, but one group has 60% accuracy, is the model "90% good"?
  • Macro-averaging treats all classes equally. When is that fair? When is it unfair?
  • An F1 of 0.8 could come from many different precision-recall combinations. Does that matter?
  • When executives ask "how good is this model?" what should you actually tell them?

Try: Show the same model evaluated by multiple metrics. Discuss how each tells a different story.

Perspective-Taking / Ethics

4. The Confusion Matrix as Moral Inventory

Each cell of the confusion matrix represents a different kind of error. Behind each number is a person with a different experience. What are their stories?

  • What's it like to be a false positive in a criminal prediction system? A false negative?
  • In medicine, a false positive leads to needless treatment; a false negative means missed disease. Which is worse?
  • The matrix makes errors look equivalent within cells. Are all false positives equally bad?
  • If you could only minimize one cell, which would you choose? Why?

Try: Have students role-play being in different cells of the confusion matrix from a real system.

Metacognition / Systems Thinking

5. Multiclass: When Reality Escapes Categories

Multiclass classification assumes examples belong to exactly one category. But the world resists neat categorization. How do we handle multiplicity?

  • A song is both "rock" and "pop." A patient has two diseases. How does classification handle this?
  • Multi-label vs. multiclass: when should a system allow multiple labels?
  • Classification systems reify categories. Does Netflix's "genre" system change how we understand film?
  • What happens when cultural categories don't translate? (e.g., emotion classification across cultures)

Try: Have students categorize ambiguous examples and notice where they disagree.

Synthesis / Application

6. ROC Curves and the Illusion of Choice

ROC curves show all possible trade-offs. But having options doesn't mean we know how to choose. What guidance do we actually have?

  • The ROC curve shows what's possible. Who decides what's desirable?
  • AUC of 0.95 vs. 0.90: Is one model "better"? Does it depend on where on the curve you operate?
  • If two stakeholders want different points on the ROC curve, how do you resolve the conflict?
  • Could you explain an ROC curve to a non-technical decision-maker? Should you have to?

Try: Give an ROC curve with marked points. Have groups choose a point and justify it to stakeholders.

Discussion Facilitation Tips

  • Think-Pair-Share: Give 2 min to think, 3 min in pairs, then class discussion.
  • Devil's Advocate: Assign students to argue positions they disagree with.
  • Real Stakes: Tie abstract questions to real systems (medical AI, hiring tools, self-driving cars).
  • Discomfort is Learning: The best discussions happen when there's no clear right answer.
  • Return to Fundamentals: Circle back to technical content after abstract discussion—"How does this change how you'd build a model?"
