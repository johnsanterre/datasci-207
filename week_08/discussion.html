Module 8: Discussion Topics

Higher-Order Thinking for Class Engagement

These discussion prompts are designed to move beyond technical definitions toward critical analysis, synthesis, and evaluation. Use them to spark deeper conversation about why we seek structure without labels.

Philosophy / Epistemology

1. Finding Structure Without Ground Truth

In unsupervised learning, there are no "correct answers." We find patterns, but how do we know if they're real or artifacts? What does "real" even mean here?

  • If a clustering algorithm finds 5 groups, were those groups "there" before we looked?
  • Astronomers found constellations. Are constellations "real"?
  • Could two valid clusterings of the same data completely contradict each other?
  • How do we distinguish pattern discovery from pattern imposition?

Try: Show the same data clustered by different algorithms. Discuss which is "right."

Analysis / Ethics

2. The Politics of Clustering People

Clustering customers, patients, or students puts people into groups they didn't choose. What are the implications of algorithmic categorization?

  • If a marketing algorithm puts you in "budget-conscious shoppers," does something change about you?
  • How is algorithmic clustering like and unlike social categories (race, class, gender)?
  • Who should have access to cluster assignments? Should individuals know their cluster?
  • Can clustering reinforce stereotypes, even if the algorithm is "just finding patterns"?

Try: Have students describe how they'd feel being assigned to different cluster names.

Synthesis / Critical Thinking

3. Choosing K: The Number of Groups

How many clusters exist is not inherent in the data—it's a choice. What does this reveal about the nature of categories?

  • The elbow method suggests K, but there's often no clear elbow. What then?
  • Is there a "true" number of human personality types? Music genres? Diseases?
  • More clusters = more precision. Fewer = more simplicity. How do we balance?
  • Could the "right" number of clusters depend on what we plan to do with them?

Try: Show silhouette scores for K=2 to K=10. Have groups argue for different values of K.

Perspective-Taking / Application

4. Dimensionality Reduction as Compression

PCA reduces 1000 dimensions to 10 by keeping "what matters most." But who decides what matters? What's lost in compression?

  • PCA keeps directions of maximum variance. Is variance always the right criterion?
  • When we compress faces to 50 principal components, are we still representing people?
  • Compression sacrifices detail for tractability. What's the analog in human cognition?
  • If the discarded dimensions contain minority representation, what are the ethics?

Try: Show PCA reconstructions at different numbers of components. Discuss what's lost.

Metacognition / Philosophy

5. What is Similarity?

Clustering requires defining similarity. But similarity is not objective—it depends on which features matter. How does this shape what we find?

  • Are a whale and a fish similar? It depends on whether you measure genes or appearance.
  • Euclidean distance treats all features equally. When is that appropriate?
  • Could two similarity measures produce opposite conclusions about group membership?
  • How do humans judge similarity? Is it consistent?

Try: Ask students to rank similarity of pairs. Note where they disagree.

Evaluation / Systems Thinking

6. Unsupervised Learning as Exploration

Unsupervised learning is often used for exploration before prediction. But exploration can lead us to see patterns that aren't there. How do we stay honest?

  • If you explore data and then test hypotheses on the same data, what could go wrong?
  • Is unsupervised learning more or less prone to confirmation bias than supervised?
  • How should exploratory findings be communicated? With what caveats?
  • "I don't know what I'll find, but I'll know it when I see it"—is this science?

Try: Run clustering on random data. Note how compelling the "patterns" look.

Discussion Facilitation Tips

  • Think-Pair-Share: Give 2 min to think, 3 min in pairs, then class discussion.
  • Devil's Advocate: Assign students to argue positions they disagree with.
  • Real Stakes: Tie abstract questions to real systems (medical AI, hiring tools, self-driving cars).
  • Discomfort is Learning: The best discussions happen when there's no clear right answer.
  • Return to Fundamentals: Circle back to technical content after abstract discussion—"How does this change how you'd build a model?"
