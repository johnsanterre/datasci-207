How to Read ML Papers

Throughout this course, you will encounter links to foundational research papers. Reading academic papers is a skill that improves with practice. This guide offers strategies to help you read efficiently and extract key insights.

The Three-Pass Approach

Do not try to read a paper linearly from start to finish. Instead, make multiple passes with different goals.

First Pass: Survey (5-10 minutes)

Get a high-level understanding of what the paper is about.

  • Read the title, abstract, and introduction
  • Read all section headings
  • Look at figures and tables (often the most informative part)
  • Read the conclusion
  • Glance at the references to see what work it builds on

After this pass, you should be able to answer: What problem does this paper solve? What is the main contribution? Is this paper relevant to what I need to learn?

Second Pass: Comprehension (30-60 minutes)

Understand the main ideas without getting lost in details.

  • Read the paper more carefully, but skip complex proofs or derivations
  • Annotate key points in the margins
  • Pay attention to figures and understand what each one shows
  • Note terms or concepts you do not understand for later lookup
  • Summarize the main method in your own words

Third Pass: Critical Reading (1-2 hours, if needed)

Deep dive for full understanding.

  • Work through the math and derivations
  • Question assumptions and identify limitations
  • Think about how you would implement the method
  • Consider how this work connects to other things you know

What to Focus On

Section What to Extract
Abstract Problem, method summary, key result (one sentence each)
Introduction Motivation, why existing solutions are insufficient
Related Work Context and positioning (often skimmable on first read)
Method The core contribution; focus here
Experiments What datasets, baselines, and metrics were used
Results Key numbers and comparisons; look at tables and figures
Conclusion Summary and future directions

What to Skip (Initially)

  • Detailed proofs (unless you need them)
  • Exhaustive related work sections
  • Appendices and supplementary material
  • Implementation details that are not central to understanding

Taking Notes

For each paper you read, try to write down:

  1. Problem: What problem is being solved?
  2. Key Insight: What is the main idea or contribution?
  3. Method: How does it work (in your own words)?
  4. Results: What are the main findings?
  5. Limitations: What are the weaknesses or open questions?
  6. Relevance: How does this connect to what I am learning?

Dealing with Difficulty

It is normal to not understand everything on the first read.

  • Accept that some parts will be unclear initially
  • Look up unfamiliar terms and notation
  • Read cited papers if you need more background
  • Discuss with classmates or instructors
  • Return to the paper after learning more context
