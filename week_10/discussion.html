Module 10: Discussion Topics

Higher-Order Thinking for Class Engagement

These discussion prompts are designed to move beyond technical definitions toward critical analysis, synthesis, and evaluation. Use them to spark deeper conversation about why CNNs revolutionized computer vision and what that means for AI.

Philosophy / Analysis

1. Why Do Convolutions Work for Vision?

CNNs exploit spatial locality—nearby pixels matter more than distant ones. But why does this assumption hold, and when might it break down?

  • Humans recognize objects regardless of position. How do CNNs achieve translation invariance?
  • Are there visual tasks where local patterns don't matter? (Abstract art? Optical illusions?)
  • CNNs were inspired by the visual cortex. Is this biomimicry or coincidence?
  • Would aliens with different eyes invent different architectures?

Try: Show images where global context matters more than local features. Discuss whether CNNs would struggle.

Ethics / Critical Analysis

2. The Transfer Learning Paradox

Pre-trained models on ImageNet enable powerful vision systems. But ImageNet was built from a particular worldview. What comes along for the ride?

  • ImageNet categories include dog breeds but not vegetables from non-Western cuisines. What's included and excluded?
  • If models learn "chair" from Western furniture, will they recognize chairs from other cultures?
  • Transfer learning democratizes AI (anyone can use), but who controls the foundation models?
  • Should there be different pre-trained models for different cultures or contexts?

Try: Test a pre-trained model on images from different cultural contexts. Discuss the results.

Synthesis / Epistemology

3. What Do Filters "See"?

We visualize learned filters and say "this detects edges" or "this detects eyes." But is that interpretation accurate, or are we projecting human concepts?

  • When we label a filter as an "edge detector," are we discovering or inventing that interpretation?
  • Deeper layers have "higher-level" features. What does "higher-level" mean?
  • Can we trust feature visualizations? Do they show what the network actually uses?
  • If two filters produce similar outputs for different reasons, does it matter?

Try: Show filter visualizations and have students generate alternative interpretations.

Systems Thinking / Ethics

4. The Compute Cost of Vision

Training large vision models requires enormous computational resources. What are the implications for who can do AI research?

  • If only large companies can train foundation models, what happens to academic research?
  • Training GPT-4V reportedly cost $100M+. Who benefits from such investments?
  • Environmental cost: large models have significant carbon footprints. How do we weigh benefits and costs?
  • Does efficient research become more valuable as compute costs rise?

Try: Calculate the approximate cost of training a model. Discuss who can afford it.

Perspective-Taking / Speculation

5. Pooling as Information Loss

Pooling deliberately throws away information for the sake of invariance and efficiency. What's lost, and does it matter?

  • Max pooling keeps the strongest signal. What if subtle signals matter?
  • In medical imaging, could pooling discard diagnostically relevant details?
  • Strided convolutions are replacing pooling. Does this solve the problem or just hide it?
  • Is there a philosophical parallel—summarizing always loses something?

Try: Show an image at different resolutions. Discuss what's lost at each level.

Evaluation / Application

6. When CNNs Fail

CNNs are brittle in ways humans are not. Small perturbations (adversarial examples) can completely fool them. What does this reveal about their "understanding"?

  • Adversarial patches can make a stop sign invisible to self-driving cars. How worried should we be?
  • Humans and CNNs fail differently. Does this mean they "see" differently?
  • If we don't understand why CNNs fail, can we trust them in critical applications?
  • Should adversarial robustness be a requirement for deployment?

Try: Generate simple adversarial examples. Discuss what makes CNNs vulnerable.

Discussion Facilitation Tips

  • Think-Pair-Share: Give 2 min to think, 3 min in pairs, then class discussion.
  • Devil's Advocate: Assign students to argue positions they disagree with.
  • Real Stakes: Tie abstract questions to real systems (medical AI, hiring tools, self-driving cars).
  • Discomfort is Learning: The best discussions happen when there's no clear right answer.
  • Return to Fundamentals: Circle back to technical content after abstract discussion—"How does this change how you'd build a model?"
